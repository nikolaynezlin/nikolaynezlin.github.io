Stormwater plumes from satellite imagery, 2005-2015

 
 

Satellite imagery is a useful source of information about the extension of stormwater plumes, which are a primary source of pollution in the southern California nearshore waters.

This is the plume of Santa Clara River in southern California.
MODIS-Terra image 02/29/2004; 18:50 UTM, 3 days after rainstorm.
Yellow dots show the stations where CTD profiles and samples were collected.

This image demonstrates that the polluted area significantly exceeded the sampled zone near the river mouth.

The water precipitated in the watershed is the main factor regulating the plume area. The effect of other factors (remote wind forcing, tides) is small. The relationship between the rainstorm magnitude and the plume size depends on land-use characteristics of the watershed.

Effect of wastewater discharge on phytoplankton in southern California coastal ocean (Unpublished study, 2017-2018)

 
 

In this study, we address the following questions:

  • How much anthropogenic nutrient pollution contributes to phytoplankton growth in highly populated arid regions like southern California?
  • Is phytoplankton biomass over the outfalls higher than in other shelf areas?

In southern California (SC), four Publicly Owned Treatment Work (POTW) facilities discharge large amount of ammonia nitrogen (6,800–14,700 metric tons per year each), significantly exceeding other anthropogenic sources.

Major submerged sewer outfalls in SC:

  • City of Los Angeles’ (CLA) Hyperion Treatment Plant

  • Los Angeles County Sanitation District (LACSD) Joint Water Pollution Control Plant

  • Orange County Sanitation District (OCSD)

  • City of San Diego (CSD) Point Loma Water Treatment Plant

The direct effect of effluent on phytoplankton biomass and the role of discharged nutrients in primary production still need to be quantified.

Data:

CHL profiles collected during routine water quality monitoring around four outfalls (quarterly, since 1998).


Method:

For each observed period:

  1. CHL profiles interpolated to 3D grids

  2. CHL concentrations transformed into the rate of nitrogen consumption by phytoplankton using a “balanced growth” model:
    • C:CHL = 50:1
    • C:N = 6.625
    • Phytoplankton growth rate = 1 / day

  3. Daily nitrogen consumption rates were compared to nitrogen discharged from the outfalls

Result:

  • Wastewater discharge is sufficient to sustain phytoplankton growth in the areas 3-5km from the outfalls (3-8% of the total shelf area of southern California).

  • Direct effect of effluent on phytoplankton biomass was small but statistically significant

  • Over all four outfalls, median CHL concentrations were significantly higher as compared to the reference regions of similar size

  • Total increase of phytoplankton biomass was much lower than the volume of discharged nitrogen

  • Most pronounced effect was observed in semi-enclosed Santa Monica Bay (CLA outfall) – 11.7% of effluent nitrogen remains as phytoplankton biomass

  • Least pronounced effect of effluent were observed over open narrow shelf (LACSD outfall) and over deep CSD outfall – 1.7%-2.7%

Trends in chlorophyll concentration and vertical structure in the Southern California Bight during recent 30 years, 2015-2018

 
 

Previous studies demonstrated increase in surface chlorophyll concentration along the California coast, which may be a result of either eutrophication or climatic cycles.

In this study, we address the following questions:

  • Do increasing trends in CHL affect only nearshore areas or offshore zone as well? If the second is true, it cannot be explained by eutrophication.
  • Are trends at the surface related to vertical structure of phytoplankton?
  • Did increasing trends continue after 2010-2012?

Data:

Ecosystem characteristics collected by CalCOFI program and averaged over the observed period (1984-2015).

CHL biomass and vertical structure, isopycnal depth and spiciness were also analyzed nearshore on the data collected during water quality monitoring around major wastewater outfalls

Are the observed trends statistically significant?​​

Method of detection statistically significant trends: [Simpson, 2014]

Empirical Orthogonal Functions (EOFs) with missing data reconstruction (DINEOF) decompose time series of spatial maps into a set of orthogonal functions (modes). These modes consist of (1) maps of spatial distributions of a variable for each mode; and (2) a time series that quantifies how each map changes in time.

 

Normalized amplitude functions (Principal Components) transformed to dimentional amplitude functions with removed seasonality

 
<

Smoothed by Generalized Additive Models (GAMs)

Significant trends are the periods when the derivative (slope) is significantly (p<0.05) different from zero

 

For almost all metrics, the trends changed direction between 2006-2010

CHL concentration was increasing and shoaling before 2006-2010 and decreasing and deepening after.

 

Trends in CHL biomass were correlated with availability of nutrients.

Similar trends were observed nearshore.


Trends in Chlorophyll-a in the SCB offshore and nearshore were associated with large scale climatic cycles:


  • In the northeastern Pacific Ocean, the periods of positive (‘‘warm’’) PDO phases are associated with an El-Nino-like increase of sea surface temperature, deepening of the pycnocline, and a decrease of primary production

  • NPGO is associated with the strength of the California Current, transporting productive ocean waters from the north

These large-scale factors affected nutrient availability. Their effect on the physical structure of the water column may also influence the delivery of nutrients from deep ocean outfalls to the euphotic zone, making it difficult to distinguish the effects of anthropogenic inputs on chlorophyll along the coast.

Using Pix4D software, 3D model is produced from images and georegistered using Ground Control Points




Objects in 3D model can be measured, including distances, areas and volumes

 

By removing small objects from 3D model, Digital Elevation Model (DEM) of terrain can be produced

 



Surface can be classified by its color. Normalized Digital Vegetation Index (NDVI) is used to quantify vegetation by measuring the difference between near-infrarted (which vegetation strongly reflects) and red light (which vegetation absorbs)


Over water, standard software like Pix3D cannot merge images, because the surface is unstable and tie points cannot be automatically selected.
​
Instead, I made a MATLAB code transforming images to georeferenced rasters using the image EXIF metadata, correcting them for distortion and merging several rasters into one.




Nearinfrared image (790 nm) taken by Sequoia camera.

EXIF metadata includes:

  • Image position (latitude, longitude, altitude)
  • Orientation (heading, pitch and roll)
  • Lens distortion parameters
 


MATLAB code transforms each pixel from image coordinates to world coordinates, resulting in georeferenced raster.

 

Several georeferenced rasters can be merged to one large raster using available manual tie points (seashore, buoys, pilings, etc.).

Unfortunately, this project was stopped due to funding problems. Hopefully, in future I will have a chance to continue this work.

Data_visualization
 

Data visualization tools built using R-Shiny technology

These interactive tools were built using R-Shiny technology for visual analysis of oceanographic data collected during water quality monitoring around major wastewater outfalls in southern California.

First tool helps analyzing a typical oceanographic dataset, collected using Conductivity-Temperature-Depth profilers.

Start the application in new window:      CTD profiles 

In the center you see the vertical profiles collected in Fall 2005 around the Orange County Sanitation District outfall. You can see the profiles of Temperature (TdegC), Salinity (Spsu), Specific Density (Sigma), Colored Dissolved Organic Matter (CDOM), Chlorophyll concentration (CHL) and Dissolved Oxygen (DO).

Select the parameter you would like to see (TdegC, Spsu, Sigma, CDOM, CHL or DO) and the vertical scale (Depth or Density).

To zoom in, brush and double-click in the rectangle.

To zoom out, double-click anywhere in the plot area.

To the left, you see the map of stations where profiles were collected. You can zoom in and out and move the map with the mouse pointer.

To the right, you see the table of stations. You can browse it and search stations by their IDs. Also, clicking triangles to the right of the Stn_ID column you can sort the stations by their IDs in alphabetic order. Clicking triangles to the right of the Date/Time column you can sort the stations in the order they were collected.

Looking at the profile which is different from the others, you can click it, making the profile red and seeing the station ID in the legend and the station at the map as a large red circle. To unselect, click anywhere in the plot.

If you wish to analyze the profiles in certain area, select one station at the map, making the station marker and the profile green. To unselect, click anywhere in the map.

If you want to select several stations by their IDs, click them in the table and see their profiles and locations in blue.

To unselect all selected stations and profiles, click the Clear button below the table.

The same data look great as a 3D plot.

Start the application and see the wastewater plume as a high CDOM concentration around the outfall:      3D plot 

To see Temperature (TdegC), Salinity (Spsu), Chlorophyll (CHL), Colored Dissolved Organic Matter (CDOM) and Dissolved Oxygen (DO), select the parameter from the drop-down menu.

Using sliders, you can change the viewpoint (height and azimuth), the depth of horizontal (Z) slice and the location of vertical north-to-south (NS) slice.

Using slider to the right, you can change the color scale.

This is just an example demonstrating how these visualization tools could be used for the analysis of different kinds of oceanographic data, the results of automatic plume detection and modeling of nearshore processes.
